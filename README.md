**California's ghost gun recoveries surged for nearly a decade, then started to fall — and a handful of Southern California counties drove almost all of it.**

**Data Source:**

The numbers come from the California Department of Justice, which tracks guns recovered by law enforcement in its Automated Firearms System. I did not get them directly from the state; I used a cleaned version published by The Trace’s Gun Violence Data Hub, a nonprofit newsroom that extracted some figures from deep in state reports and obtained the rest through public records requests. This provenance is worth being upfront about. The raw counts come from law enforcement agencies, and the compilation is from an outlet whose mission is to report on gun violence, funded by foundations such as Arnold Ventures and the MacArthur Foundation. Even if you share that mission, a dataset is not neutral just because a nonprofit or government agency produced it. Both have reasons for collecting what they collect. The bigger caveat is what the data cannot see. It only counts guns that were recovered and reported, so it measures law enforcement activity as much as it measures ghost guns. The state also suppresses any county count under 10 for privacy reasons. It notes that recent years are revised upward as agencies enter backlogged records. This means 2024’s apparent dip may partly reflect incomplete data rather than a real decline.

I used Google Sheets. The raw file broke down ghost gun recoveries by county and year, so little cleaning was needed. I checked that county names were spelled consistently, year columns were in order, and blank cells weren’t read as zeros. This matters because the state suppresses any count under 10, so a blank doesn’t mean “none.”

​

The main pivot has counties in the rows and sums recoveries for each year, with a grand total row and column. Two things stood out. First, the statewide trend shows a clear rise and fall: recoveries increase from just 3 in 2013 to a high of 10,880 in 2021, then fall each year to 7,442 in 2024\. Growth is not slow. It’s relatively flat until 2019, then it explodes, tripling from 2019 to 2020 alone. Second, the totals are highly concentrated. Los Angeles has by far the most recoveries of any county, with 10,448, about 23% of the state’s total. That’s followed by San Bernardino (6,398), San Diego (4,199), and Riverside (2,602). All three top counties are in Southern California and together account for almost half of all ghost guns recovered in the state over the entire period.

One caveat I kept in mind while reading these numbers is that they are raw counts, so the largest counties are partly just those with the largest populations. Los Angeles is big, which helps it lead. That’s a limitation of what a count can show, so a per-capita comparison would be the natural next step to a fuller story.

**Ending Summary**

This project aimed to clarify the timeline and geographic distribution of the ghost gun issue in California. The data presents a two-part case: California law enforcement agencies recovered hundreds of untraceable homemade firearms in the early 2010s, followed by thousands beginning in 2017\. Recoveries peaked at 10,880 in 2021 and declined over the subsequent three years. Most recoveries were concentrated in Los Angeles County, which accounted for approximately one-quarter of the statewide total, and in several Southern California counties, which together accounted for the remainder.

​

It is important to recognize the limitations of the data. These figures represent recovered and reported firearms, not exclusively ghost guns, so the numbers reflect law enforcement activity as much as the actual prevalence of untraceable firearms. A county with higher recoveries may simply have more proactive policing, more officers trained to identify ghost guns, or superior record-keeping, rather than a greater number of such firearms in circulation. Los Angeles's high total partly reflects its large population and intensive policing, not necessarily a more serious per capita problem. Additional limitations include the state's suppression of county counts below ten for privacy reasons, resulting in some small rural counties reporting zeros that may not represent true zero recoveries; these were treated as 'no reported recoveries above the threshold.' Furthermore, the Department of Justice cautions that recent years' data are often revised upward as backlogged records are processed, so the observed decline after 2021, particularly in 2024, may be attributable to incomplete data rather than an actual decrease.

​

The data does not directly explain why 2021 marked a turning point, but several plausible factors merit consideration. The surge in recoveries coincides with the pandemic period, during which overall gun purchases increased, and build-at-home kits provided a rapid, unregulated means of obtaining firearms. The subsequent decline aligns with the introduction of regulatory and enforcement measures, including federal rules classifying unfinished kits as firearms requiring serialization, California's ban on unserialized guns, and legal actions that reportedly led to the closure of the nation's largest ghost gun manufacturer following a settlement with Los Angeles. These explanations remain hypotheses rather than definitive conclusions. To substantiate any of these factors, it would be necessary to cross-reference the data with the implementation dates of these policies and legal actions.

​

A significant ethical concern is the potential for geographic stigma. County rankings may be misinterpreted as indicators of dangerousness, when in fact they primarily reflect areas of concentrated enforcement. This misperception can disproportionately affect urban and lower-income communities that already experience heightened policing. To mitigate this risk, I emphasized statewide trends, consistently clarified that raw recovery counts are influenced by population size and law enforcement practices rather than gun ownership, and avoided equating high-recovery counties with high-crime areas. For a more comprehensive analysis, I would normalize the data by population to provide a per capita perspective, consult with the Department of Justice and local law enforcement regarding recovery reporting practices, and compare the timing of observed trends with the implementation of relevant laws and legal actions.
